Autonomous Vehicles

An autonomous car is a vehicle capable of sensing its environment and operating without human involvement. A human passenger is not required to take control of the vehicle at any time, nor is a human passenger required to be present in the vehicle at all. An autonomous car can go anywhere a traditional car goes and do everything that an experienced human driver does. Between 1920 and 1980 many efforts had been made by various car companies and Universities to pioneer autonomous vehicles. One of the first demonstrations was a radio-controlled driver-less car in the 1920’s. This still required a second car behind to send out radio signals to the transmitting antennae that was installed in the ‘driver-less’ vehicle in front (The Milwaukee Sentinel 1926). A few decades later, people considered driver-less cars that could be activated by electronic devices embedded in the roadway. This would mean construction of new electronically controlled streets; these were considered in the UK and parts of the US. There are different levels of vehicle autonomy – from complete driver control to completely automated with no driver input. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) in America have put forward the following classification for autonomous vehicles.

~ No-Automation (Level 0): At all times, the driver has completed and sole command and control of the vehicle with respect to steering, braking, throttle and motive power

~ Function-specific automation (Level 1): Some specific control functions such as electronic stability control or pre-charged brakes is(car) automated

~ Combined function automation (Level 2): At least two main control functions such as adaptive cruise control in combination with lane centring are automated

~ Limited self-driving automation (Level 3): Under certain traffic or environmental conditions, the driver cedes full control of all safety – critical functions and relies heavily on the vehicle to watch for any changes in conditions requiring transition to driver control. The driver will be required to resume control of the vehicle, but with sufficient transition time

~ Full self-driving automation (Level 4) The vehicle is intelligently designed to monitor roadway conditions and act solo, performing all safety–critical driving functions for an entire trip (a fully driverless level)

Autonomous vehicles rely on sensors, algorithms, machine learning systems, and powerful processors to execute software. Autonomous vehicles create and maintain a map of their surroundings based on a variety of sensors situated in different parts of the vehicle. Radar sensors monitor the position of nearby vehicles. Video cameras detect traffic lights, read road signs, track other vehicles, and look for pedestrians. Lidar (light detection and ranging) sensors bounce pulses of light off the car’s surroundings to measure distances, detect road edges, and identify lane markings. Ultrasonic sensors in the wheels detect curbs and other vehicles when parking. Sophisticated software then processes all this sensory input, plots a path, and sends instructions to the car’s actuators, which control acceleration, braking, and steering. Hard-coded rules, obstacle avoidance algorithms, predictive modelling, and object recognition help the software follow traffic rules and navigate obstacles.

A number of car companies have predicted the fully autonomous vehicles will be on the market within the next 5-10 years - this includes Audi in 2017 (Torr, 2014), Ford in 2020 (Su, 2015), Nissan in 2020 (Nissan, 2013) and Tesla in 2023 (Kaufman, 2014). Google is probably most advanced, and they plan to have a driverless car in the market by 2018 (Tam, 2012).

Everything has positive and negative impacts from its inception, and autonomous vehicle technology is no exception. The future era of driverless cars will bring us the following five advantages.

1. Safety: Studies show that approximately 94% of car crashes are caused by human error, resulting in more than 1 million deaths worldwide each year. With the advent of new partially autonomous vehicles, especially those equipped with collision warning systems, some crashes have been avoided.

2. Cost savings: Car accidents cause billions of dollars in economic damage each year, especially in terms of property damage and medical costs. For car owners and taxpayers, a reduction in injury accidents and fatalities would naturally reduce these costs significantly, as taxpayers pay taxes that are primarily used to pay for medical expenses and road construction costs.

3. Environment: Driverless cars will be able to travel more efficiently on the road than cars operated by human drivers, which will lead to a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, specifically for a number of reasons. First, the reduced number of accidents will help reduce traffic congestion on the road. Vehicles will also likely communicate with road infrastructure (e.g., traffic signals), which in turn will adjust fuel consumption and emissions accordingly.

4. Transportation services: Cab services, car sharing, and public transportation will become faster and cheaper as driverless vehicle changes advance. As mentioned earlier, as traffic congestion decreases and navigation becomes more efficient, vehicles will be able to reach their destinations faster.

5. Time Savings: With driverless cars on the market, navigation will be more efficient, traffic congestion will be reduced, and because cabs and car-sharing services will be cheaper than purchasing a car, there will be fewer cars on the road, which will ultimately improve commuting efficiency.

On the negative side, autonomous vehicle technology can lead to unemployment for taxi drivers and bus drivers, the acceptance of this new technology and how to develop appropriate laws. Unemployment will be a problem in the short and medium term and will require government support and security in exchange for social stability. People's acceptance of this new technology depends on safety and trust issues, if people's lives are not safe or in danger, it will be difficult to accept this technology in daily life. Finally, the government needs to consider how to take responsibility for accidents and penalties if an autonomous vehicle is involved in an accident.

First, it will affect my means of travel, for example, because of the massive popularity of autonomous vehicles, he will replace the current bus and taxi, so my travel is completely dependent on autonomous vehicles. Cloud servers came into fruition in the 1960s by Joseph Carl Robnett Locklider, he was a phycologist and computer science that wanted to connect people all over the world. By the 2000s, technology powerhouse companies such as Amazon and Microsoft were introducing their own cloud computing services (Educba).

Modern cloud services allow companies to make resources accessible to people over a remote network or online interface. We may not realise how often we use these things in our everyday life, from school to work and even at home, these services make things such as accessing school resources via a school portal, working remotely, and syncing your information across all your devices. This means that the cloud servers are the heart of any cloud computing service, and they can be delivered in multiple ways, Infrastructure as a Service, Platform as a Service or Software as a Service and Function as a Service, the platform can also be deployed in different ways depending on the enterprise’s preferences. They can be deployed in a public way the third part provider makes the resource available to the general public over the internet, an example of this service is the Google Cloud. It can be also deployed in a private way where an enterprise will use a proprietary architecture and will run the cloud with its own data centre, the enterprise will have their own hardware on premises and have all control of underlying cloud infrastructure. Lastly, they can also be deployed in a hybrid way which combines features of both public and private.

Cloud severs come into existence through a process called virtualisation which makes use of a software called a hypervisor also known as a virtual machine monitor, this software creates and supports multiple virtual machines by creating one host computer capable of running multiple guest virtual machines. It divides and shares the resources of the host computer such as its memory and processors. In a public setting the resources are split and used by multiple organisations and/or individuals, with dedicated private servers, owners or renters are provided with their own physical server which the hypervisor virtualises but does not split the resources.

As technology continues to evolve, these virtual machines will likely become much more secure, with security systems becoming more integrated with AI and machine learning, this will help automate all the processes of threat prevention as well as risk detection. One of the largest growing platforms for the cloud service is serverless computing also known as Function as a Service, this is where developers do not need to manage the cloud server and all they need to do is write and deploy their application code.

Machine Learning

What does it do?

Learning by machines is a subfield in artificial intelligence. The evolution of artificial intelligence follows a clear and logical progression from "reasoning" through "knowing" to "learning." Evidently, machine learning is a method for achieving artificial intelligence, i.e., it is utilised to address issues in artificial intelligence. In the last 30 years, machine learning has evolved into a multidisciplinary area incorporating probability theory, statistics, approximation theory, convex analysis, and computational complexity theory, among others. The construction and study of algorithms that enable computers to "learn" automatically is the subject of machine learning theory. Machine learning algorithms are a family of algorithms that automatically examine data to identify patterns and then use those patterns to draw inferences about unknown data. Due to the substantial amount of statistical theory involved in learning algorithms, machine learning has a tight relationship with inferential statistics and is also known as statistical learning theory. Machine learning theory is concerned with established, implementable learning algorithms in terms of algorithm design. Numerous inference issues come under the category of no-procedure-to-follow difficulty, hence machine learning research includes the development of simple approximation techniques.

Machine learning generates algorithms that enable computers to better comprehend data and make decisions based on it. Some analysts predict that machine learning will become widespread by 2024, with particular focus on the years 2022 and 2023. Data mining, computer vision, natural language processing, biometric recognition, search engines, medical diagnosis, credit card fraud detection, stock market analysis, DNA sequence sequencing, speech and handwriting recognition, strategic gaming, and robotics have all utilised machine learning extensively. There are a range of learning models using distinct algorithms within machine learning. Learning models may be divided into four categories based on the nature of the data and the intended outcome: supervised learning, unsupervised learning, semi-supervised learning, and reinforcement learning. Moreover, one or more algorithms may be used to each model based on the dataset employed and the desired outcomes. The primary functions of machine learning algorithms include classification, discovery of patterns, result prediction, and the making of informed choices. Generally, algorithms are employed alone; however, if the data being processed is very complicated and unexpected, numerous algorithms may be combined to enhance accuracy.

Ethereum merger

These days, Ethereum will usher in a merger. What is an Ethereum merger? The Ethereum Foundation gives a precise definition of the term: "A merger represents the combination of Ethereum's existing execution layer (the main net we use today) and its new proof-of-stake consensus layer, the Beacon Chain." More precisely, it is said that the beacon chain (consensus layer) is merged with the existing chain (execution layer), and the PoW part of the original execution layer is discarded. The completion of this merger means the end of the Ethereum POW consensus era, and the POW node miners will withdraw from the historical stage.


PoW may seem perfect, but what is most easily criticized and criticized is the energy consumption necessary to ensure network security. As the computing power of the entire network increases, energy consumption also increases rapidly. On the development planning roadmap of Ethereum, the PoW mechanism will gradually withdraw from the historical stage, and Ethereum will transition to PoS (Proof of Stake) and gradually phase out the PoW mining mechanism. It should be emphasized that the energy and computing power consumed by PoW create the security barrier of the network - the 51% attack principle determines the desire to attack a system with strong computing power (systems such as Bitcoin and Ethereum also carry considerable Wealth of volume), it must be uneconomical in terms of cost. Therefore, an economic constraint can be reached between network wealth value, network computing power, and network security. However, the corresponding price is energy consumption (which will be under pressure under the social consensus of green energy saving) and the threshold for participating in mining. PoS is anticipated to lower the network's energy usage by at least 99.95% when compared to Ethereum's present resource-intensive PoW governance scheme.


The entire upgrade process of Ethereum is divided into three steps: 1) beacon chain, 2) merge, 3) shard network. And it is currently in the critical second step- the merger - from pow to pos.


Merge has long been in the development plan of Ethereum. In order to smooth the transition, the beacon chain was first introduced on PoW. However, in the face of mature PoW miners and a developed mining machine industry, the merger is not easy, and the differences of interests and technical obstacles it faces are also obvious. In order to make the established PoS route go smoothly and make the transition as low as possible, the latest development state on the eve of the merger is Ethereum 2.0. By introducing the beacon chain (ETH2.0), it runs based on the underlying PoW block and runs in the letter. The development of sharding on the standard chain.


The merged block data structure remains unchanged, and the useless fields are set to 0 or equivalent. After merging, several fields that were previously included in the proof-of-work block header are no longer helpful. However, in order to minimize disruption to tools and infrastructure, these fields are entirely removed from the data structure and are instead set to 0 or the corresponding Equivalent value. In this way, the impact of the merger is minimized for application implementations and developers.


Additionally, it will open the door for sharded chains in 2023, which are anticipated to remove data congestion, high gas (transaction) costs, and support layer 2 scaling systems of the next generation. The Ethereum Foundation claims that sharded chains give rollups and applications a second, less expensive storage layer for data storage.


Why Ethereum should be upgraded

There are two main reasons for the upgrade of Ethereum: 1) the pow mining mode requires a lot of energy consumption, which is not conducive to environmental protection; 2) the current Ethereum network is over-congested, and the high gas fee limits the user experience, and Affect the development of the project party.


This time the Ethereum merger is mainly a solution to the first reason. The pos mechanism can completely change the energy consumption of mining. It is worth mentioning that "merging" cannot solve the problem of high gas, and the sharding technology proposed for high gas, which is the third step of the Ethereum upgrade, will be implemented in 2023.

Developers are trying to remove features that can cause delays and temporarily limit the ability to withdraw staked ETH when the merger is complete in order to speed up the transfer. However, a combined "clean" update might be able to fix problems.
